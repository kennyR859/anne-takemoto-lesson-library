Mixed Review: Logarithms → Polynomials

Student: Anne Takemoto Date: 2026-04-24
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSF-BF.B.5 / HSN-CN.C.9 — Apply the inverse relationship between exponents and logarithms; extend polynomial identities to the complex numbers using the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
Part 1 — Logarithms (4 problems)
Directions: Show every step. For Problem 4, state the domain restriction and reject any extraneous solutions.
1. Use the change-of-base formula to evaluate \(\log_5(28)\). Give the exact form (as a quotient of common or natural logs) and a decimal approximation rounded to 4 decimal places.
2. Expand fully using the laws of logarithms (no logs of products, quotients, or powers should remain): \[ \log_3\!\left(\dfrac{x^{2}\sqrt{y}}{z^{4}}\right) \]
3. Condense into a single logarithm: \[ 3\ln(x) \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(y+1) \;+\; 2\ln(z) \]
4. Solve for \(x\). State the domain first, solve algebraically, and reject any extraneous solutions. \[ \log_2(x-3) \;+\; \log_2(x+1) \;=\; 5 \]
Part 2 — Exponential Modeling (3 problems)
Directions: Set up the model first, then solve. Round final numerical answers to 2 decimal places unless otherwise stated.
5. A principal of \(\$4{,}500\) is invested at an annual rate of \(4.2\%\), compounded continuously. Using \(A(t) = Pe^{rt}\), determine how many years it will take for the investment to grow to \(\$10{,}000\).
6. A radioactive sample decays according to \(A(t) = A_0\,e^{kt}\), where \(t\) is in years.
(a) If the sample's half-life is 28 years, find the exact value of \(k\) (leave it in terms of \(\ln\)).
(b) Using your value of \(k\), find how many years it takes until only \(12\%\) of the original sample remains.
7. Convert between exponential bases. Rewrite \(f(t) = 600\,(1.035)^{t}\) in the form \(f(t) = 600\,e^{rt}\).
(a) Find the exact value of \(r\).
(b) Interpret \(r\) as a continuous growth rate (state as a percentage to 2 decimal places).
Part 3 — Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (2 problems)
Directions: Use the Conjugate Root Theorem. Show your division work.
8. Given that \((2 - i)\) is a zero of \(P(x) = x^{4} - 5x^{3} + 7x^{2} + 3x - 10\), find all four zeros and write \(P(x)\) in fully factored linear form.
9. Find a polynomial \(P(x)\) of degree 4 with real integer coefficients having zeros \(x = 3\) (multiplicity 2) and \(x = 1 + 2i\). Expand your answer to standard form \(ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e\).
Part 4 — Synthetic Division & Factor Theorem (2 problems)
Directions: Use synthetic division. For #10, fill in missing terms with zero coefficients.
10. Use synthetic division to divide \(\bigl(3x^{4} - 8x^{3} + 5x - 7\bigr) \div (x - 2)\). State the quotient \(Q(x)\) and remainder \(R\). Then verify your remainder by computing \(P(2)\) directly (Remainder Theorem).
11. Find the value of \(k\) so that \((x + 4)\) is a factor of \(P(x) = 2x^{3} + kx^{2} - 11x - 12\). Justify your answer using the Factor Theorem.
Part 5 — Polynomial Graphing (1 problem)
Directions: Use multiplicity rules (bounce / cross / slide) and end-behavior analysis.
12. Given \(P(x) = -(x+1)^{2}(x-2)^{3}(x-4)\):
(a) State the degree and the end behavior (use arrow notation \(\uparrow / \downarrow\)).
(b) List all zeros with their multiplicities and classify each as bounce, cross, or slide.
(c) Find the \(y\)-intercept.
(d) Sketch a rough graph showing all the above features clearly.
