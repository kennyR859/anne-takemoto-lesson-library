Solving Trigonometric Equations

Student: Anne Takemoto Date: 2026-05-12
Standard: CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSF-TF.B.7 — Use inverse functions to solve trigonometric equations that arise in modeling contexts; evaluate the solutions using technology, and interpret them in terms of the context.
🔔 Mr. Kenny's Key Point

Two big traps in trig equations:

1. Always check the range (\(|\sin| \leq 1\), \(|\cos| \leq 1\)) — extraneous roots like \(\cos x = 3\) must be rejected.

2. For multiple-angle equations (\(2x\), \(x/2\), etc.), find ALL solutions in the wider domain first, then translate back to \(x\).

Part A — Direct Equations & Factoring
Directions: Solve each equation on the interval \([0, 2\pi)\) unless stated otherwise. Show all work in the workspace provided.
1. Solve \(2\sin x - 1 = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
2. Solve \(\tan x + 1 = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
3. Solve \(2\sin\theta\cos\theta + \cos\theta = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Hint: Factor out a common trig factor first.
4. Solve \(\sin^2 x - \cos^2 x = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Hint: Divide both sides by \(\cos^2 x\) or use the Pythagorean identity.
Part B — Quadratic in a Trig Function
Directions: Let \(u\) = the trig function, factor, then solve. Watch for extraneous roots!
5. Solve \(2\cos^2 x - 7\cos x + 3 = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Let \(u = \cos x\). Factor and reject any root outside \([-1, 1]\).
6. Solve \(2\cos^2\theta + \sin\theta = 1\) on \((0, 2\pi]\).
Use the identity \(\cos^2\theta = 1 - \sin^2\theta\) to convert to a quadratic in \(\sin\theta\).
7. Solve \(\tan^2 x = 3\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Part C — Multiple-Angle Equations
Directions: For multiple-angle equations, first solve for the compound angle over an extended domain, then convert back to \(x\).
8. Solve \(\cos 2x = -1\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Let \(u = 2x\). Solve for \(u\) in \([0, 4\pi)\), then halve.
9. Solve \(\sqrt{3}\,\tan\!\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right) - 1 = 0\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Isolate \(\tan(x/2)\), solve for \(x/2\) in \([0, \pi)\), then double.
10. Solve \(\sin 2x = \dfrac{1}{2}\) on \([0, 2\pi)\).
Let \(u = 2x\). Solve for \(u\) in \([0, 4\pi)\), then halve. Expect four solutions.
